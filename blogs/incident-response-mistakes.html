Common Mistakes During Incident Response

Aug 30, 2024 7 min read

When a major cyber incident occurs, chaos often takes over. IT teams scramble to restore services, while security teams scramble to contain the threat. In this rush, critical mistakes are often made.

1. Restarting Compromised Machines

The classic IT troubleshooting step—"have you tried turning it off and on again?"—is a disaster for Incident Response. Restarting a machine clears volatile memory (RAM), destroying evidence of running malware, decrypted passwords, and active network connections. It can also trigger malware logic that encrypts the drive upon reboot.

2. Failing to Isolate the Network

Instead of pulling the power plug, responders should pull the network cable (or isolate the VLAN). Leaving a compromised machine connected to the network allows ransomware to spread laterally or attackers to exfiltrate data while you attempt triage.

3. Modifying Evidence

Opening suspect files, running antivirus scans, or logging into compromised accounts alters file timestamps, registry keys, and event logs. All triage and evidence gathering must be done forensically, preserving the original state.

4. Lack of Communication Plans

If an attacker has compromised your email server, using corporate email to discuss the incident response plan gives the attacker a front-row seat to your defense strategy. Organizations must have out-of-band communication channels (like Signal or separate tenant Slack spaces) established beforehand.

Preparation is the key to effective incident response. Establishing playbooks and conducting tabletop exercises ensures that when a real incident happens, the team acts with precision rather than panic.
